About the Agency

Our mission in Arcadia Planitia

The A.R.C.A.N.A. Space Agency coordinates a multi-decadal effort to transform shallow Martian subsurfaces into biologically conditioned mantles, starting from the ice-rich volcanic domes of Western Arcadia Planitia. Here, subsurface ice, thermal buffering and basaltic regolith offer one of the most promising starting points for biological regolith engineering beyond Earth.

Strategic objectives

A.R.C.A.N.A. develops, tests and deploys modular bioreactors that can thrive in the chemically oxidizing, desiccated and nutrient-poor environment of the Martian near-surface. By leveraging extremophilic microbial consortia, we aim to:

Detoxify regolith by reducing perchlorates and other reactive oxidants.
Capture and slowly release water from sublimating subsurface ice.
Enhance bio-available nutrients through controlled weathering of basalt.
Build a stable, biologically active mantle to support robotic agriculture and, later, human habitats.

Mission architecture

The A.R.C.A.N.A. architecture is deliberately modular. SMM pods are deployed by rovers into pre-selected domes, where they remain sealed and self-contained while still exchanging gases, vapours and heat with the surrounding regolith. This balances biosecurity with long-term in situ terraforming.

Subsurface Microbial Module

The engineered bioreactor at the heart of A.R.C.A.N.A.

The Subsurface Microbial Module (SMM) is a vertically stratified bioreactor that houses extremophile consortia. Its architecture captures vapour from sublimating ice and redistributes it through micro-porous interfaces that slowly rehydrate a dormant payload of microorganisms, enabling long-term, low-rate microbial activity in the Martian subsurface.

Engineering & Operations

SMM deployment is handled by autonomous rovers operating inside selected Arcadia domes. Each pod is emplaced a few meters below the surface, in contact with shallow ice-bearing layers. Onboard telemetry provides low-bandwidth monitoring of internal pressure, temperature and key metabolic indicators.

  • Fully self-contained bioreactor with replaceable cartridges.
  • Modular arrays allow fine control of biological loading per dome.
  • Failsafe modes include cold-trapping and chemical passivation.
  • All microbial strains are selected from naturally extremophilic lineages and remain confined to the pod.

Target performance: measurable reduction in perchlorate content and improved water retention within a few meters of each SMM after < 10 Martian years of operation.

Terraforming Roadmap · 2025–2090

Five-phase mission pathway

A.R.C.A.N.A. follows a staged strategy, from initial robotic deployment to the activation of autonomous neo-habitats. Each phase builds on the biological and geochemical legacy of the previous one.

Phase 1
Pioneer SMM Deployment
c. 2025–2035

Robotic scouts emplace the first generation of SMM pods in a subset of Arcadia domes, validating drilling, emplacement and biosecurity protocols.

Phase 2
Microbial Mantle Formation
c. 2035–2050

Expanded arrays of SMM pods create overlapping spheres of influence, gradually forming a biologically conditioned mantle beneath the domes.

Phase 3
Enhanced Microbial Guilds
c. 2050–2070

Additional functional guilds are introduced within the pods to improve nutrient cycling, carbon storage and soil structure while remaining confined.

Phase 4
Robotic Greenhouses
c. 2070–2080

Surface domes host fully robotic greenhouses anchored into the conditioned mantle, testing crop performance and closed-loop life-support components.

Phase 5
Neo-habitat Activation
c. 2080–2090+

Selected domes transition into neo-habitats: semi-autonomous stations with robust soil, stable water reservoirs and the infrastructure needed to support future human missions.

Background & Scientific Rationale

Why Western Arcadia Planitia?

Mars’ surface is chemically oxidizing, desiccated and hostile to long-term biological activity. Yet Western Arcadia Planitia hosts shallow subsurface ice and positive-relief volcanic domes, which together provide thermal buffering, mechanical stability and episodic availability of liquid water.

From hostile regolith to living mantle

Daily sublimation cycles in Arcadia create transient films of water around ice-rich layers. These niches resemble terrestrial endolithic habitats and offer a realistic starting point for biologically assisted regolith conditioning.

A.R.C.A.N.A. does not aim at global terraforming. Instead, it focuses on creating localized, controllable pockets of habitability — stepping stones for science, technology demonstrations and, eventually, exploration.
  • Study long-term microbial survival under Martian radiation and low pressure.
  • Quantify how biotic processes reshape porosity, chemistry and water retention.
  • Benchmark different extremophile consortia for in situ resource utilization.
  • Inform planetary protection policy with real, confined field data.
